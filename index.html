Phase 1 United States

Where TurtleSync focuses right now

TurtleSync is in an early rollout focused on the United States. The first goal is to make it easier for people on beaches, boats, and coastal communities to report a turtle in trouble without searching through many different websites.

  • Reports are collected through a single form, then routed by region and type of incident.
  • Priority areas include Florida, the Gulf Coast, the Southeast Atlantic, Hawaii, and select Pacific sites.
  • Long term, the project aims to collaborate with aquariums, rescue centers, and stranding networks across all major nesting zones.

Data and impact over time

Why a centralized hub matters

From scattered hotlines to one clear starting point

Right now, many people search phrases like “who do I call for a hurt sea turtle” and end up lost in state by state links. TurtleSync aims to provide one clear starting point. Reports can be sorted by location and incident type so they can reach the right responders faster.

As the project grows, TurtleSync can also support research. Anonymous data on time, location, and conditions can help track nesting success, common stranding hotspots, and long term trends.

  • Collect consistent fields such as GPS location, photos, tide conditions, and suspected cause.
  • Create a record that can be shared with permitted organizations without exposing personal contact details.
  • Build visual dashboards over time so patterns in strandings and nest disturbances are easier to see.

How TurtleSync started

Student led project with a long term goal

Built from a mix of volunteer work and frustration

TurtleSync grew out of years spent volunteering with animals and researching marine conservation. I saw how much time and effort goes into saving one injured animal. At the same time, I noticed how confusing it can be for the public to figure out who to call or where to report something they see on the beach.

I am a high school student planning to continue into veterinary technology and marine science. This project is designed to grow with me through college, moving from a basic reporting hub into a tool that real teams can rely on in day to day work.

Read more about the founder

My name is Ethan D’Amore. I have spent my high school years balancing dual enrollment classes, a 4.0 GPA, and hands on animal care. I volunteer at my local humane society, organized hurricane relief efforts, and created a student initiative called Hearts for Hounds to bring more teen volunteers into shelter work.

TurtleSync represents the same idea on a larger scale: build systems that make it easier for people to care for animals, especially in stressful moments when clear guidance matters most.

Ways organizations can connect

For aquariums, rescue centers, and educators

TurtleSync is actively looking for feedback from people who work with sea turtles, marine mammals, and coastal wildlife. The goal is to support existing protocols, not replace them, and to route reports to the right contacts in each region.

  • Review the reporting form and suggest changes that match your intake process.
  • Explore the idea of regional email routing or secure data exports for your team.
  • Collaborate on outreach so beach visitors know how to respond when they see a turtle in trouble.

If you represent a rescue organization or aquarium and would like to share feedback, you can reach out through the contact information listed in my college and scholarship materials, or through any future contact link added to this site.
