How reporting works

TurtleSync is built to stay simple. A report should feel quick and safe for the person and for the turtle.

  • Observe from a distance. Do not touch or crowd the animal.
  • Open the report form. Share the time, location, and what you see.
  • Add photos if you can. Images help responders understand the situation.
  • Follow local instructions. Always follow posted signs and rules on the beach.

As TurtleSync grows, the reporting process can be refined with input from people who work with sea turtles every day.

Inside a TurtleSync report

  • Date and time of sighting
  • Beach name or access point
  • Type of sighting, such as injured turtle or hatchlings
  • Short description of behavior or injuries
  • Immediate danger status
  • Optional photo or video link

These fields can change in the future if conservation partners request new information.

Sea turtle activity and data

Sea turtles in the United States are monitored through nest counts, stranding reports, and long term research. Many reports come from members of the public who notice something unusual and choose to speak up.

Common situations include:

  • Strandings after cold snaps, storms, or strong currents
  • Injuries from boat strikes or fishing gear
  • Disoriented hatchlings affected by artificial lights on the beach
  • Nesting turtles disturbed by people, vehicles, or pets

TurtleSync is meant to sit beside existing hotlines and reporting systems by giving the public one clear place to share what they see in a structured way.

More about the project

TurtleSync started in high school and is designed to keep growing through college and beyond. The sections below share more detail about the project and its direction.

About TurtleSync

TurtleSync grew out of a simple question: how can one person turn concern for sea turtles into something practical that helps others report what they see. The project focuses on clear reporting, organized data, and communication that is easy for the public to use.

About the founder

My name is Ethan D'Amore. I am a student who plans to study animal care and marine conservation. I have experience volunteering with animal shelters, completing veterinary assistant training, and helping organize community projects related to animal welfare and disaster relief.

TurtleSync is one of the ways I connect my long term goals with real work right now. The project is built to grow as I learn more from courses, field work, and collaboration with professionals.

Long term direction

Over time, I hope to develop TurtleSync in three main directions:

  • Refine the reporting form with input from conservation groups and educators
  • Explore ways to share reports with regional partners in a responsible and secure way
  • Build simple tools that help visualize where and when reports are happening

The goal is for TurtleSync to stay practical and useful as my education and experience grow, not to remain a one time project.
