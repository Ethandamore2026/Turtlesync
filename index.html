How reporting works

TurtleSync is built to be simple. A report should feel quick and safe for the person and for the turtle.

  • Observe from a distance. Do not touch or crowd the animal.
  • Open the report form. Share the time, location, and what you see.
  • Add photos if you can. Images help responders understand the situation.
  • Follow local instructions. Always follow posted signs and rules on the beach.

Over time the form and process can be adapted with input from rescue groups and marine biologists.

Inside a TurtleSync report

Current fields include:

  • Date and time of sighting
  • Beach name or access point
  • Type of sighting, such as injured turtle or hatchlings
  • Short description of behavior or injuries
  • Immediate danger status
  • Optional photo or video link

These fields can be adjusted in the future if conservation partners request new data points.

Where TurtleSync is used

Right now TurtleSync is designed for beaches in the United States, especially areas where sea turtles are commonly seen near the shoreline or nesting sites.

The long term goal is to build a simple model that could be adapted for other coastal regions around the world with guidance from local experts and rescue organizations.

  • Current focus: United States coastal areas
  • Future goal: share the idea with global conservation partners

More about the project

TurtleSync is still in development. The sections below share more detail about how it started and where it is going.

About TurtleSync

TurtleSync began as a student project that combined a love for marine life with an interest in simple, practical technology. Instead of building a complicated app, the first version focuses on getting the basics right: a clear form, an easy to understand website, and a process that can grow with real feedback.

About the founder

My name is Ethan D'Amore. I am a high school student who plans to study animal care and marine conservation. I have experience volunteering with animal shelters, completing veterinary assistant training, and helping organize community projects related to animal welfare and disaster relief.

TurtleSync is my way of taking the next step, using what I have learned about structure, reporting, and communication to support sea turtle protection in a real and focused way.

Future plans

In the future I hope to:

  • Test the reporting form with volunteers and educators
  • Invite feedback from conservation groups and marine rescue teams
  • Explore basic data dashboards for viewing patterns in reports
  • Adapt the system for different regions as opportunities allow
