Coverage and nesting hotspots

Phase 1 United States

Where TurtleSync focuses right now

The first version of TurtleSync is focused on sea turtle nesting and stranding hotspots along the United States coastline. The idea is to give people one clear starting point instead of dozens of different websites and phone numbers.

  • Reports are collected through a single form and tagged by region and type of incident.
  • Priority areas include Florida, the Gulf Coast, the Southeast Atlantic, Hawaii, and select Pacific sites.
  • Long term the project aims to partner with aquariums, rescue centers, and stranding networks across all major nesting zones.

Data and impact over time

Why a centralized hub matters

From scattered hotlines to one clear starting point

Right now many people search things like “who do I call for a hurt sea turtle” and end up lost in state by state links. TurtleSync aims to provide one starting point so people can act quickly and feel confident they are helping instead of guessing.

Over time anonymous data on time, location, and conditions can help researchers spot patterns in strandings and nest disturbances, and support outreach where it is needed most.

  • Collect consistent fields like location, photos, lighting conditions, and suspected cause.
  • Protect personal contact details while still giving responders what they need to follow up.
  • Build simple dashboards that can show trends for rescue teams, aquariums, and educators.

How TurtleSync started

Student led project with a long term goal

Built from volunteer work and real frustration

TurtleSync grew out of years spent volunteering with animals and researching marine conservation. I saw how much coordination goes into rescuing even one injured animal. At the same time I noticed how confusing it can be for the public to figure out who to call when they see a turtle in trouble.

This project is designed to grow over the next several years, moving from a simple reporting hub into a tool that real teams can rely on during nesting season and storm events.

Read more about the founder

My name is Ethan D’Amore. I have spent high school balancing dual enrollment courses, a 4.0 GPA, and hands on animal care. I volunteer at my local humane society, organized hurricane relief efforts, and created a student initiative called Hearts for Hounds to bring more teen volunteers into shelter work.

TurtleSync follows the same idea on a larger scale. I want to build systems that make it easier for people to care for animals, especially in stressful moments where clear guidance matters most.

For aquariums, rescue centers, and educators

Help shape the next version

TurtleSync is actively looking for feedback from people who work with sea turtles, marine mammals, and coastal wildlife. The goal is to support existing protocols and make it easier for the public to reach the right team quickly.

  • Review the reporting form and suggest changes that match your intake process.
  • Explore ideas for regional routing, email notifications, or secure data exports.
  • Collaborate on outreach so beach visitors know what to do when they see a turtle in trouble.

Contact details will be added as formal partnerships develop. For now this site serves as a working prototype and a starting point for conversation with future partners.
