Behind TurtleSync

How a student project became a real conservation tool

TurtleSync started as an idea on paper and grew into a working platform with a custom domain, reporting workflow, and a plan for real partnerships. This page explains how it was built, what research went into it, and where it is heading next.

1. Origins

Where the idea came from

My interest in sea turtles began long before I wrote a single line of code. I have spent years following rescue stories, visiting aquariums and turtle hospitals, and learning how strandings and nesting are monitored along the coast. At the same time, I was volunteering with shelters, earning a veterinary assistant certification, and planning a future in veterinary technology and marine animal care.

One pattern kept standing out. When a turtle is in trouble, the public often does not know exactly who to call or where to report what they see. Different states and counties have different hotlines and websites. Important details can be missed or never recorded at all. TurtleSync grew from a simple question.

What if there was one clear place to start that captured good data and supported the teams already doing the work?

2. Research

Learning how real strandings and nesting reports work

Before building anything, I spent time studying how sea turtle response actually operates. I looked at:

  • How state agencies and permitted groups handle reports of stranded or injured turtles.
  • What information they request from callers, such as exact location, visible injuries, and behavior.
  • How nesting beaches are monitored and how volunteers are trained to avoid disturbing nests.
  • Common causes of strandings, including cold stun, boat strikes, entanglement, and light disorientation.

I also studied public facing educational materials about what to do and what not to do if someone finds a turtle, a nest, or lost hatchlings. This helped shape the language on the reporting form and the educational content on the main site.

3. Building the first version

From notes to working site and report flow

Technical foundation

The first public version of TurtleSync is built as a static site hosted through GitHub Pages with a custom domain at TurtleSync.org. I used:

  • HTML and CSS for a clean, mobile friendly layout with clear sections and calls to action.
  • A custom hero image and hotspot map graphic to visually connect the project to sea turtles and US coastlines.
  • Google Forms as the initial reporting backend to collect and structure incoming reports.

This approach makes the site fast, accessible, and easy to update as I learn more and as partners share feedback.

Design decisions

Every part of the layout was chosen with a purpose.

  • The homepage focuses on what the user needs most: how to report, what the platform does, and where it operates.
  • Deeper content about my story and the technical details lives on separate pages so the main experience stays simple.
  • Buttons are clear and consistent so that even a stressed beachgoer can find the right action quickly.
4. Field guide content

Helping people recognize what they see on the beach

A key part of TurtleSync is education. Many people are not sure what they are looking at when they see a nest, a stranded turtle, or a confused hatchling near street lights. The content I am building into the platform focuses on:

  • What a marked sea turtle nest typically looks like and why it is important not to disturb it.
  • Basic signs that a turtle may be stranded or injured, such as difficulty moving, obvious wounds, or entanglement.
  • Common cold stun scenarios in colder water, and why turtles may appear lethargic.
  • How artificial lights can disorient hatchlings and how the public can reduce that risk.

This educational layer supports better reports and also builds respect for local laws and permitted responders. The goal is to help people assist without accidentally doing harm.

5. Roadmap

Where TurtleSync is heading next

The current version of TurtleSync is a working prototype. As I move into veterinary technology training and later into marine science, I plan to grow the project step by step.

Planned stages

  • Refine the reporting form based on feedback from rescue staff and volunteers.
  • Move from simple form storage to a structured database with better filtering and export tools.
  • Create regional views that help permitted organizations see only the cases that fit their coverage area.
  • Develop yearly summary reports that highlight trends in strandings, nesting, and public reports.

Each stage will be guided by input from professionals and by what is realistic for a student developer to maintain responsibly.

6. Personal growth

What this project has taught me so far

Living with autism, I rely on structure, systems, and clear steps to handle complex work. Those same skills are what allowed me to design TurtleSync, stay organized, and keep moving through each stage of development while balancing school, dual enrollment courses, and animal care volunteering.

Building this project has helped me:

  • Translate a conservation problem into a practical tool that anyone can use.
  • Think in terms of workflows and data rather than only ideas.
  • Practice communicating with different audiences, from beachgoers to potential research partners.
  • See how my long term goal in animal care and marine conservation can connect directly to technology and data.

TurtleSync is not a finished product. It is the foundation of the work I want to keep doing through college and beyond, supporting animals that cannot ask for help themselves and the people who dedicate their lives to protecting them.
